Myself, MOST (Multi-Organ Segmentation Tool) is an advanced AI solution tailored for precise segmentation of organs in CT scans. It leverages two distinct models: UNET, which specializes in capturing localized features for accurate segmentation of specific anatomical structures, and UNTER, which harnesses transformer-based architectures to analyze long-range dependencies within the images. By integrating these models, MOST achieves comprehensive segmentation, accurately delineating organs and structures across the entire image, ensuring high accuracy and reliability in medical imaging applications.

Real-life application

Disease Diagnosis and Monitoring

Accurate segmentation of organs allows for better visualization and assessment of abnormalities, tumors, or lesions within those organs. Once organs are segmented, quantitative analysis can be performed to measure various characteristics of pathological regions, including size, shape, volume, and spatial distribution.

Treatment Planning:

Organ segmentation helps in treatment planning for surgeries, radiation therapy, and other interventions. By precisely delineating the boundaries of organs and tumors, surgeons can plan surgical approaches and minimize damage to surrounding healthy tissue. In radiation therapy, organ segmentation assists in calculating optimal radiation doses and ensuring accurate targeting of tumors while sparing healthy organs.

Image-Guided Interventions

During minimally invasive procedures such as laparoscopy or robotic surgery, organ segmentation provides real-time guidance to surgeons, allowing them to navigate within the body and manipulate surgical instruments more accurately. This enhances surgical precision, reduces operative time, and minimizes the risk of complications

Medical Education and Research:

Segmented medical images serve as valuable educational tools for medical students, residents, and practicing healthcare professionals to better understand human anatomy and pathology. They also facilitate research endeavors, enabling quantitative analysis of anatomical structures, disease progression, and treatment outcomes.
